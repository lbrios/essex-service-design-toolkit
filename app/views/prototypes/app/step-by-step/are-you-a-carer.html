Part of Adult social care: step by step

Are you a carer?

A 'carer' is someone who - without being paid - regularly looks after, helps or supports someone over the age of 18 who wouldn't be able to manage everyday life without their help.

Carers under the age of 18 are sometimes called 'Young Carers'.

Who you may be looking after

You may be caring for a partner, relative, friend or neighbour. For example, it may be someone who:

  • has a physical or learning disability
  • has a mental health condition
  • is living with dementia
  • has sight, hearing or communication difficulties
  • has a long-term health condition
  • misuses drugs or alcohol

A carer doesn't have to be living with the person they care for, and the help they give doesn't have to be physical.

Things you may help do

You may be helping with:

  • shopping, personal finances
  • paperwork and administration
  • transportation
  • personal care, such as washing and dressing
  • going to the toilet or dealing with incontinence
  • eating or taking medicines
  • getting about at home or outside
  • practical things, like companionship
  • counselling, emotional support or communicating
  • attending appointments

Part of Adult social care: step by step

  1. 1
    Get help yourself

    There are lots of ways you can get help and support yourself with day-to-day living.

    1. Talk to family and friends
    2. Visit a doctor – go to the NHS website to find out how
    3. Find walking and mobility aids
    4. Get help with meals and shopping
    5. Adapt your home to work for you
    6. Use technology, like alarms and monitors, to keep you safe
    7. Join a club or support group
    8. Care Choices can help you find care services.
    9. The Care Quality Commission has guidance on finding care services and care homes.
    10. If you are looking after someone, see support for carers.

  2. 2
    Contact Adult Social Care

    The first thing you need to do is to have a conversation with adult social care about your needs and what could help you manage everyday tasks.

    They will ask you about what’s important to you, what you can manage yourself, what additional support you may need and how you’re feeling about things.

    They will agree an action plan with you to help you achieve the outcomes that are important to you.

    Call Adult Social Care

    Telephone: 0345 603 7630
    Textphone: 0345 758 5592

  3. 3
    An assessment to see if you might be eligible for support

    A care needs assessment is a more detailed conversation of your care and support with a social worker or occupational therapist. It will identify your eligible needs and what support you could get.

    You can have someone with you during the assessment. This could be your family, friends, carer or an advocate to speak on your behalf.

    Your carer is also entitled to a carer’s assessment.

  4. and
    Costs of social care

    Most people have to pay something towards their care and support.

    How much you will be asked to pay depends on your income and assets.

    See our guidance on paying for care and support.

  5. 4
    Care and support planning

    Following your assessment and agreement of your eligible needs, you will receive a care and support plan that will detail your identfied needs and how they will be met. It will include:

    • What your desired outcomes are
    • Which outcomes are eligible for Adult Social Care support
    • What your personal budget is and how it's going to be spent

    Age UK has guidance on finding and arranging social care.

    Disability Rights UK has guidance on care needs assessments and support planning.

  6. 5
    Regular review of care needs

    Over time people’s care needs change so there might be changes in care and support.

    We will review your support plan to make sure it's still meeting your needs.

    Let us know if your care needs or your circumstances have changed as this may affect your care and support plan.
